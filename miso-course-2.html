Your five misses, dissected

Each one: what you picked, why it tempts, and the fix.

MISS №1 · A2‖x − c‖² = ∫(x−c)² ds is best read as…

✗ you picked: "the unsigned area between the curves"

✓ the SQUARED gap, accumulated over the domain

Unsigned area is ∫|x−c| ds — the L¹ distance. Squaring is different in a way that matters here: big local gaps count disproportionately. A curve that deviates hugely in one small region is "farther" in L² than one with the same total area of small deviations. Since the GFM feeds this squared distance into an exponential, one region of bad mismatch kills a rule's membership fast — that's a design property, not an accident.

MISS №2 · C2Estimating β(s,t) without any penalty is…

✗ you picked: "fine — least squares is unbiased"

✓ ill-posed: infinitely many unknowns, finite data

The unbiasedness instinct is from finite-dimensional regression with more samples than parameters. A slope surface has infinitely many degrees of freedom, so infinitely many β's fit the training curves exactly — and they behave wildly between data points. §2 lets you watch this happen with a slider.

MISS №3 · D2If ONE input's membership ≈ 0, the rule…

✗ you picked: "still fires if the other memberships are high"

✓ barely fires, no matter the others

Firing uses a product across the N inputs: 0.95 × 0.95 × 0.02 ≈ 0.018. Any single input can veto the whole rule — that's what makes the product an AND. If the paper had used, say, the average, one matching input could carry a rule whose other inputs look nothing like the prototype. §3 has the two-slider demo.

MISS №4 · E1A GFM is fully specified by…

✗ you picked: "a centre curve and a radius curve" — and you did NOT mark unsure

✓ a centre curve c(s) and a SCALAR radius ν

This is the most valuable miss of your test precisely because you were confident. The radius νk,n is one number per (rule, input) — the cluster's standard deviation, eq (3.1.3). 01

The eigen engine

Block G, rebuilt from the ground. After this, Theorem 3.2.3 → eq (3.2.42) should read like a recipe, not a wall.

1.1 · What an eigenvector is, honestly

A symmetric matrix A acts on directions: feed it a unit vector ξ, and the number ξᵀAξ tells you how much A "rewards" that direction.

For a covariance matrix, the reward is concrete: ξᵀΣξ = the variance of the data projected onto direction ξ. Eigenvectors are the directions where the reward peaks (and troughs); eigenvalues are the reward values there.

1.2 · The Rayleigh quotient, live

The Rayleigh quotient R(ξ) = ξᵀΣξ / ξᵀξ is just "reward per unit length" — so it depends only on the direction. Rotate ξ through 180° and watch:

FIG 1rotate ξ, read the quotient
R(ξ) = —

Left: a data cloud and your direction ξ (vermillion arrow). Right: R(θ) — the projected variance. The peak sits exactly at the leading eigenvector (dashed): λ₁ = 3.92 at θ ≈ 27°. No search needed — the maximiser of a Rayleigh quotient IS the top eigenvector. That fact is the entire engine of §III-B.

1.3 · Why a SEQUENCE Φ₁, Φ₂, … your FT3

your diagnostic said "it captures more of the input. They differ." Here is the version to own:

One direction produces ONE score per sample — a single number. But the response S(t) is a whole curve; one number cannot carry it. So we extract directions one at a time:

  • Φ₁ = the direction whose scores explain the response best (top of the quotient).
  • Φ₂ = the best direction among those whose scores are uncorrelated with z₁ — that is the orthogonality constraint's whole job. It forces Φ₂ to explain something new.
  • …continue until L = 10 directions. Same logic as PC1, PC2, PC3 in ordinary PCA.

So Φ₂ differs from Φ₁ not just numerically — it is the answer to a different, constrained question: "best remaining direction."

FIG 2successive directions, PCA-style

Vermillion: the best direction (Φ₁). Teal: the best direction uncorrelated with it (Φ₂). In 2-D the constraint pins Φ₂ to the perpendicular; in the paper's high-dimensional coefficient space, a whole subspace remains each round.

1.4 · The generalised quotient and the Cholesky trick eq (3.2.40)–(3.2.42)

The paper's quotient has Ψ, not I, in the denominator:

maxξ ξᵀ Σ ξξᵀ Ψ ξ   where  Σ = covariance with the response,  Ψ = input variance + penalties

Read it as: signal per unit of penalised cost. A direction that correlates with y but is wiggly or huge pays for it in the denominator.

To solve it, factor Ψ = CᵀC (Cholesky — possible because λ > 0 makes Ψ positive definite) and substitute u = Cξ:

ξᵀΨξ = ξᵀCᵀCξ = (Cξ)ᵀ(Cξ) = uᵀu   ⇒   maxu uᵀ A uuᵀ u,   A = (Cᵀ)⁻¹Σ C⁻¹

The denominator became the plain length of u — we are back to Fig 1's standard quotient, whose maximiser is a plain eigenvector. This is called whitening: C stretches the coordinate system so the "cost ellipse" Ψ becomes a circle. Solve for u, map back with ξ = C⁻¹u. With the affine orthogonality constraints kept along, this is the ACRQ problem, solved by eigenvectors of a projected matrix 𝒫A𝒫.

1.5 · Why closed-form beats an iterative optimiser your FT4

your diagnostic said "…gradient descent could trap in some local optimum and eigen solution won't" — that instinct is correct, keep it. The full list you can now give a reviewer:

  • Exact global optimum — eigendecomposition doesn't get trapped; your point.
  • Deterministic & reproducible — no random initialisation, no seeds; rerunning gives bit-identical consequents.
  • No tuning of the optimiser itself — no learning rate, no convergence threshold, no early stopping to justify in review.
  • Constraints hold exactly — orthogonality is built into the solver, not approximated by a penalty.
  • Known cost — 𝒪((HNK)³), small here; and it is why the identification has a clean complexity claim in §III-E.

1.6 · The full pipeline, mapped to the paper

  • Response expansion: S(t) = Σ zᵢwᵢ(t) — KL, best L-term truncation (3.2.3)
  • Scores are input functionals: zᵢ = ⟨𝒳, Φᵢ⟩ with θₖ inside the operator (3.2.6)–(3.2.8)
  • Basis-expand φ = δᵀe(s) → everything becomes matrices Σ, Ψ, Λ in ξ (3.2.32)–(3.2.39)
  • Maximise the generalised quotient → Cholesky whiten → eigenvectors (3.2.40)–(3.2.42)
  • Recover response shapes wᵢ(t) by penalised least squares (η) (3.2.44)–(3.2.45)
  • Assemble βk,n(s,t) = Σᵢ φk,n,i(s) wᵢ(t) (3.2.11)

Six lines. Everything in §III-B is one of these six moves plus a proof that the move is legitimate.

In the paper's quotient ξᵀΣξ / ξᵀΨξ, the numerator and denominator measure, respectively…

  • input variance / response variance
  • covariance with the response (signal) / penalised input variance (cost)
  • bias / variance
  • train MSE / test MSE

Signal per unit cost. Directions that predict y win; directions that are large or wiggly pay in the denominator.

Φ₂ is defined as…

  • the second-best unconstrained direction
  • Φ₁ rotated by 90°
  • the quotient's maximiser among directions whose scores are uncorrelated with z₁
  • the direction minimising the quotient

The orthogonality constraint changes the question: "best of what remains." That is why the sequence keeps adding new information — your FT3, now answered.

The substitution u = Cξ (with Ψ = CᵀC) works because…

  • it diagonalises Σ directly
  • ξᵀΨξ = uᵀu, so the messy denominator becomes plain length — a standard eigenproblem
  • it removes the constraints
  • C is orthogonal

Whitening: stretch coordinates until the cost ellipse is a circle. λ > 0 guarantees the Cholesky factor exists and inverts.

Which is NOT one of the closed-form solution's advantages?

  • deterministic, reproducible output
  • exact global optimum
  • constraints satisfied exactly
  • it scales better than gradient methods to millions of parameters

Cubic cost (HNK)³ would NOT scale to millions — it wins here because the dimension is small. Knowing the limits of your own method is referee-proofing.

02

Ill-posedness & the three penalties

Fixing miss №2 and turning your H-block guesses into ownership.

In classical regression (n samples ≫ p parameters), least squares is safe. Functional regression breaks the premise: a slope surface β(s,t) has infinitely many degrees of freedom, your data is always finite.

Consequence: infinitely many β's reproduce the training curves exactly. They agree on the data and disagree wildly everywhere else. The data alone cannot pick one — that is what ill-posed means.

FIG 3watch least squares fall apart — then save it
train — true —

Dashed ink: the true function. Dots: 12 noisy observations. Vermillion: the penalised least-squares fit. Push H to 24 with λ at −8: train error collapses, true error explodes — the interpolating monster. Now raise λ and watch it calm down. THIS is why C2's "least squares is fine" fails, and why every functional method carries penalties.

The paper's three knobs, now with owned meanings:

  • λ — ridge on the weight functions φ. Statistical job: tame size. Algebraic job: make Ψ positive definite so Cholesky exists (you now know both jobs).
  • τ — penalises ‖φ″‖²: curvature of the input weights. Straight lines cost nothing — the penalty targets wiggles specifically, not slopes.
  • η — penalises ‖w″‖²: curvature of the response shapes.

Too small → Fig 3's monster. Too large → oversmoothed, underfit (train AND test both bad). The paper sets all three in [10⁻⁴, 10⁻²] and shows <10% sensitivity — which you already understood (J3, confident and correct).

With 12 data points and 24 basis functions at λ ≈ 0, the fit has train error ≈ 0. Its error against the TRUTH is…

  • also ≈ 0 — it found the truth
  • large — infinitely many interpolants exist and they oscillate wildly between data points
  • exactly the noise variance
  • undefined

Zero train error just means the fit memorised the noise. Between the dots, nothing constrains it — you watched it in Fig 3.

The second-derivative penalty ‖φ″‖² leaves which functions completely unpenalised?

  • constants only
  • all straight lines a + bs
  • all polynomials
  • sinusoids

φ″ = 0 exactly for linear functions — the penalty's null space. It fights curvature, not trend. That's why it is the standard smoothness penalty in functional data analysis.

Underfitting (τ, η far too large) shows up as…

  • train ≈ 0, test large
  • train AND test both high — the model is too stiff to follow even the training data
  • train large, test ≈ 0
  • identical to overfitting

Overfit = gap between train and test (your FT6 answer, which was exactly right). Underfit = both bad, no gap. Two different diseases, two different symptoms.

3.3 · GFM precision, once and for all miss №4

Per (rule k, input n): one centre CURVE ck,n(s) — the cluster's mean — and one SCALAR radius νk,n — the cluster's standard deviation, a single number summarising spread in L² distance.

Inventory for the whole antecedent side, K rules × N inputs: K·N curves + K·N numbers. Nothing else. If someone asks "how many antecedent parameters does your model have?", that is the answer.

Memberships (0.9, 0.9, 0.01) across three inputs. The rule's raw firing is about…

  • 0.60 — the average
  • 0.9 — the majority wins
  • 0.008 — the product; the third input vetoes
  • 0.01 — the minimum

0.9 × 0.9 × 0.01 = 0.0081. Every input must match; one stranger kills the rule. (Min would give 0.01 — close here, but min ignores HOW MANY inputs are mediocre; the product compounds them.)

A test input lands far from ALL rule centres. The normalised TS system outputs…

  • approximately zero
  • the average of all rules' outputs
  • essentially the nearest rule's local model, extrapolated at full weight
  • an error flag

Normalisation forces Σθ = 1 regardless of how weak the evidence is. Silent extrapolation — check max raw membership if you need a trust signal. (Unnormalised collapse-to-zero was D3, which you had right.)

The antecedent parameters of a K-rule, N-input MISO-TS-FFS are exactly…

  • K·N centre curves + K·N radius curves
  • K·N centre curves + K·N scalar radii
  • one centre curve + one scalar per rule
  • K·N·L scalars

Redemption for E1: the radius is one number per (rule, input) — eq (3.1.3) is a standard deviation, and standard deviations are scalars.

FPCA: from correct to confident

You went 4/4 on block B and flagged every answer unsure. The knowledge is in; let's make it yours.

The picture to hold: FPCA finds modes of variation — the recurring shapes of deviation from the mean across your sample of curves.

  • The covariance operator is the curve-world covariance matrix: it records how the value at s co-varies with the value at s′ across the sample.
  • Its eigenfunctions ψp(s) are the deviation shapes; eigenvalues λp say how much of the sample's variation each shape carries (you got B4: λ₁/Σλ = "80% explained").
  • Each curve's score am,p = ⟨xm − mean, ψp⟩ says how much of shape p THIS curve contains (you got B2).
FIG 6a stylised NO₂ day, rebuilt from two modes

Dashed ink: the mean day (two rush-hour humps). Vermillion: mean + a₁ψ₁ + a₂ψ₂. Mode ψ₁ lifts or lowers the whole day; mode ψ₂ trades morning against evening.

your FT1 said "the component that explains the data the most, a feature/tendency" — right direction, too vague to defend. The upgraded sentence:

"PC1 is a curve ψ₁(s) — the dominant shape by which individual days deviate from the mean day; for NO₂ it is typically an overall-level mode separating polluted days from clean ones, and each day's score a₁ measures how much of that shape it carries."

One sentence, three owned facts: it IS a curve, it is a deviation-from-mean shape, scores quantify per-sample content.

For daily NO₂ curves, the first FPCA component is most plausibly…

  • the mean daily curve itself
  • an overall-level mode: polluted days vs clean days
  • measurement noise
  • the time axis

Level shifts usually dominate variation in environmental series, so the "how polluted overall" shape carries the largest eigenvalue. (Dataset-dependent in general — but this is the right default expectation, and the right thing to SAY when asked.)

Day m has a large NEGATIVE score am,1 on the level mode ψ₁. Day m was…

  • an average day
  • a day with strong morning rush only
  • a markedly cleaner-than-average day — it carries ψ₁'s shape in the negative direction
  • a day with missing data

Scores are signed coordinates: +a₁ pushes the day up along ψ₁, −a₁ pushes it down. You just read Fig 6's slider in reverse.
